~95 Theses Against Sandwich Nomenclature

Scenario 1


It is around noon on a gorgeous Sunday. You are outside trying to prove Goldbach's conjecture when mom calls you in for lunch. Awaiting you on the kitchen table, in all its blazing glory, is none other than a delightful turkey sandwich.


Scenario 2


You are abducted by the dictator of the Galactic Confederacy, Xenu, who reveals his plan to transport you to a volcano in his DC-8-like spacecraft and kill you with a hydrogen bomb. His alien troops start to surround you and press you from all sides very tightly. However, you manage to use your cellphone to call the head of the Space Force and cry out, "Help! I am trapped in an alien sandwich!".


What's going on here?


Now, the problem is that the two ways these scenarios refer to sandwiches is inconsistent. The first scenario refers to a sandwich by its contents, in this case the cold meats. However, the second scenario refers to a sandwich by that which does the job of surrounding the contents or holds it together. Furthermore, this is interesting because flipping the nomenclature in either scenario renders the sentence hopelessly nondescript.

That is, what if your mom had called, "Come get your bread sandwich?". Well, what is in it? Is it something you like? Maybe. Maybe not.

Similarly, what if you used your precious seconds on the phone with the head of the Space Force to plead, "Help! I am trapped in an <insert name> sandwich!". Why should the head of the Space Force be compelled to take action? There is no discernible threat.

Additionally, if the receiving party in either scenario was expecting the other nomenclature, problems may ensue. For example, the Space Force head may interpret this to be that you are doing the sandwiching, if he expects that a sandwich is named by its meat. That is, maybe you are bullying an alien by sandwiching the said alien, in which case, again, the Space Force head would not help, instead maybe he would send PETA, which I would imagine would be marvelously unprepared for the situation.


What should we do?


It is my proposal that we use "andwich" when we are attempting to describe an object which has a surrounding structure by the contents inside the surroundings, and that we use "sandwich" when we are attempting to describe an object which has a surrounding structure by the surroundings. This nomenclature mimics the definition they achieve and makes it clear in all cases what the object of interest or threat is. Under this convention, we would say, "Thanks for this ham andwich mom!" while the second scenario stays the same.

Don't agree with me? Well, as they say, it's my way, or Xenu's highway.
